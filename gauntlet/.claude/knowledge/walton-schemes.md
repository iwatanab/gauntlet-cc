# Walton Argumentation Scheme Taxonomy

Source: Walton, Reed & Macagno — Argumentation Schemes (2008)

## Argument from Sign

Warrant pattern: X is a sign of Y; X is present; therefore Y is probable.

Critical questions:
1. Is X a reliable sign of Y in this context and population?
2. Are there alternative explanations for X that do not implicate Y?
3. What is the base rate of Y given X alone?
4. Is stronger evidence available that would make X redundant?

## Argument from Expert Opinion

Warrant pattern: Expert E asserts A within domain D;
therefore A is presumptively acceptable.

Critical questions:
1. Is E genuinely an expert in domain D?
2. Is A within the scope of E's expertise?
3. Is there consensus among experts in D, or is A contested?
4. Does E have a conflict of interest?
5. Is A consistent with other authoritative sources in D?
6. Is E's opinion based on sufficient evidence?

## Argument from Analogy

Warrant pattern: C1 resembles C2 in relevant respects; conclusion holds
in C1; therefore presumptively in C2.

Critical questions:
1. Are C1 and C2 relevantly similar in the respects that matter?
2. Are there relevant differences that would defeat the analogy?
3. Is the conclusion in C1 well established?
4. Are there cases more similar to C2 that support the opposite conclusion?

## Argument from Cause to Effect

Warrant pattern: A causes B; A is present; therefore B will occur.

Critical questions:
1. Is the causal link between A and B established in this domain?
2. Are confounders present that could produce B without A?
3. How certain is the causal mechanism?
4. Is A sufficient for B, or merely contributory?

## Argument from Consequences

Warrant pattern: Action A leads to outcome B; B is undesirable;
therefore reject A.

Critical questions:
1. Does A actually cause B in the relevant conditions?
2. How certain is the causal chain between A and B?
3. What other consequences follow from A?
4. How do A's consequences compare with the consequences of not doing A?

## Argument from Practical Reasoning

Warrant pattern: Goal G is desired; action A achieves G; therefore do A.

Critical questions:
1. Are there other actions that would achieve G?
2. Is G the right goal given the circumstances?
3. Does A have side effects that compromise G or produce worse outcomes?
4. Is A possible given the constraints?

## Argument from Position to Know

Warrant pattern: Agent P is in a position to know whether A is true;
P asserts A; therefore A is presumptively true.

Critical questions:
1. Is P actually in a position to know A?
2. Does P have a reason to be dishonest about A?
3. Is A the kind of thing that can be known from P's position?
